What is POCUS?

Point-of-care ultrasound is a focused ultrasound examination performed and interpreted by a trained clinician where care is delivered.

POCUS = Point-of-Care Ultrasound. It adds real-time visual information to the clinical assessment and helps answer a defined question at the bedside.
IPA KNOWLEDGE / 01
At the point of care

The examination happens where the patient is being assessed and treated.

Focused

It is built around a specific clinical question, not an exhaustive survey of every organ.

Repeatable

A trained clinician may repeat it when the patient's condition or the clinical question changes.

Clinical scope

What can POCUS support?

Applications depend on the clinician's specialty, training, local scope of practice and available quality systems.

01

Heart and circulation

Focused assessment of cardiac activity, haemodynamics and selected causes of circulatory instability.

02

Lungs and breathing

Bedside assessment of pleural and lung patterns in the context of respiratory symptoms.

03

Trauma and abdomen

Focused searches for free fluid and selected abdominal or retroperitoneal findings.

04

Vascular questions

Selected venous and arterial assessments, including focused evaluation for deep vein thrombosis.

05

Procedural guidance

Real-time visualization for appropriately selected and supervised ultrasound-guided procedures.

06

Other focused applications

Musculoskeletal, soft-tissue, obstetric and other applications within relevant training and clinical governance.

The essential distinction

POCUS and comprehensive ultrasound are not the same examination

They can complement one another. The right choice depends on the clinical question and the level of detail required.

FeaturePOCUSComprehensive ultrasound
Primary purposeAnswer a focused question in the immediate clinical context.Systematic diagnostic evaluation of an organ, region or condition.
Where and whenAt the point of care, often during the initial or ongoing assessment.Usually in an imaging service or dedicated examination workflow.
Who integrates the resultThe treating clinician integrates images immediately with history, examination and other data.A qualified imaging professional produces a formal diagnostic interpretation for the care team.
ScopeLimited to defined views and questions appropriate to the operator's competence.Broader and more detailed, following a complete examination protocol.
Next stepMay guide immediate decisions, monitoring or the need for further imaging.Provides a comprehensive diagnostic report within the relevant specialty.

A focused examination can miss abnormalities outside its scope. When the question remains unresolved, the findings are discordant, or a complete assessment is required, additional imaging or specialist consultation may be necessary.

Clinical workflow

POCUS is a process, not only an image

Responsible use connects image acquisition with clinical reasoning, documentation and quality review.

Define the question

Start with a clinically relevant question that POCUS can reasonably address.

Acquire images

Use the appropriate transducer, views and infection-control measures.

Interpret in context

Combine the findings with the history, examination and other available information.

Act or escalate

Use the result within competence, or obtain further imaging and expert help.

Document and review

Store representative images, record findings and include the examination in quality assurance.

Limits and safety

What POCUS does not replace

Its value depends on an appropriate indication, a competent operator and honest recognition of uncertainty.

A

A complete clinical assessment

POCUS is interpreted alongside the history, physical examination, laboratory data and other investigations.

B

Comprehensive imaging

A negative or limited focused scan does not exclude every relevant abnormality.

C

Formal training

Device ownership or an online page alone does not establish competence.

D

Clinical governance

Documentation, image storage, infection control, supervision and quality assurance remain essential.

Safety note

Diagnostic ultrasound does not use ionizing radiation, but examinations should still be performed prudently, for an appropriate purpose, by trained personnel and in accordance with local policies.

Learning pathway

How to begin responsibly

  1. Learn the foundationsUltrasound physics, controls, image orientation, artefacts and safety.
  2. Complete structured trainingA curriculum matched to your specialty and intended applications.
  3. Practise with supervisionHands-on scanning, feedback and review of saved studies.
  4. Demonstrate competenceAssessment should cover image acquisition, interpretation and clinical integration.
  5. Maintain qualityOngoing practice, documentation, peer review and continuing education.
